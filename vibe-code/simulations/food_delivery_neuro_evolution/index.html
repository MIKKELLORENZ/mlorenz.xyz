How it works

Every car is driven by a feed-forward network (33 → 32 → 24 → 16 → 2) whose full set of weights and biases is its genome. It sees 14 type-blind rays — each reports only the distance to the nearest object of any kind, since walls, cars and people are all equally fatal — its speed and compass, a fictional GPS (heading error, cross-track offset, the next two turns, a red-light warning, lane position) and a dwell-progress signal that rises while a stop registers and snaps to zero when it completes — the cue to pull away again. Outputs are steering and throttle; the speed limit is generous and cruising pace is the net's own choice. There is no backpropagation anywhere.

Each generation the whole population drives the same seeded episodes at once, and car-to-car contact follows a schedule (Car contact select): in ghost episodes cars neither see nor hit each other — drawn translucent — isolating clean route driving, while contact episodes keep collision avoidance under selection; with the default 1-in-2 every generation scores both skills. Fitness is dominated by completed deliveries, then pickups (paid in proportion to the route driven to earn them), a dense progress ramp on every leg — each px the car gets closer to its current door than ever before on that leg pays out, once, whether it is fetching or delivering — route coverage, a graded approach credit for reaching a door slowly, distance, collisions and traffic law. Ending a job any way other than delivering — parked out, crashed, executed at a red — forfeits that pickup's credit: arriving alive always beats arriving fast and dead, and parking on a banked reward never beats carrying the food. Survivors breed by copy-paste crossover — contiguous slices or single genes copied directly from each parent — plus gaussian noise on a slider-chosen number of genes. Elites survive byte-identical with a grace window (slider, default 4 generations — longer grace was benchmarked slower; graced elites don't breed until they re-earn their rank) — getting rammed by another car cannot erase a good genome; only a newcomer that clearly beats an elite's record and beats it head-to-head in every episode of a generation (or expiry) ends its protection — one lucky episode evicts nobody — while a slider-chosen share of mutated elite copies — with their own mutation-spread slider — competes alongside random immigrants; a variant only takes over by genuinely out-scoring its untouched original.

Towns rotate through four layout styles — classic grid, roundabout, one-way couplet, and curved streets — with dense traffic lights. Slim one-way streets and the roundabout ring are single-lane; driving against them counts as wrong-side driving. Cars spawn evenly spread along the road network, and the scenario reshuffles every generation: fresh spawn positions, job draws and pedestrian streams each time, so leaders must re-prove themselves instead of replaying one memorized route — while within a generation every car drives the same shared scenario, keeping the ranking fair. The curriculum climbs three rungs: cars first spawn already carrying with the nearest home door as target — the drive-a-leg-then-stop endgame is trained directly — then, once the population median delivers reliably, they start empty and must reach the nearest short-route pickup first; finally jobs rotate across the whole pool and chain pickup → delivery → next pickup. Driving off the route triggers an A* replan, exactly like a satnav. Overshooting a door opens a short recovery window: the GPS aims back at the target so braking or reversing can save the stop; blowing past it counts as a miss and reroutes around the block, since U-turns are banned. A first miss costs a little fitness — repeat misses on the same stop cost 350 each, and route coverage pays at most once per leg, so circling a missed door only bleeds points. Deliveries require a genuine standstill at the door. Buildings, trees, the grass, other cars, pedestrians and red lights are all instantly fatal — but fairly so: lights carry a one-second all-red clearance (a just-flipped red is not an execution), pedestrians look both ways before stepping off the curb and bumping into a stationary car is their blunder, and lawful waiting — at a red, boxed into a queue, or yielding to a crossing pedestrian — never counts as stagnation (though a car that makes no progress at all for a full minute is retired, abandoning any carried job). In a head-on crash the wrong-side driver bears the heavier penalty (2×) while the car in its legal lane pays a reduced one (0.35×). The Eval toggle freezes evolution and lifts the time limit: the current brains simply drive until the last car is out.
