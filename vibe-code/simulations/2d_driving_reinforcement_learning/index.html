How it works

Each car senses the world with 7 distance rays plus its own speed, and also sees the nearest car ahead and behind — where they are now, where they were a moment ago, and their relative velocity, so a brain can anticipate their path. It senses incoming bullets at long range (position and velocity), and a reload neuron rises from 0 to 1 as its own gun becomes ready. Everything feeds a small recurrent neural network whose hidden state gives it short-term memory. It outputs steering, a combined throttle / brake / reverse signal, and a trigger. There is no artificial top speed — drag is the only limit, and the walls don't negotiate.

Every car carries a gun, but guns stay locked for the first 150 generations so driving skill evolves first. After that: one bullet every 2 seconds (reload timers start randomized), with a little recoil. A kill earns big bonus fitness (worth about 70 m of driving) and getting shot costs heavily; near misses earn the shooter graded credit and rattle the car they grazed, so aiming and dodging improve by degrees instead of waiting on lucky hits. Blind shots with no car in sensor range cost a little. Aggression, evasion, or pure racing: whatever wins, breeds.

Training is neuroevolution: each generation is scored over two runs from different grid slots (averaging out luck), and the fittest cars seed the next population with mutated copies of their network weights. Crashing, stalling, or driving too slowly gets a car eliminated early, and jerky steering slowly drains fitness — smooth driving is cheaper on the machinery. The fitness chart shows laps, so progress is comparable across tracks.

To test generalization, turn on Eval mode (no learning) and switch tracks — the cars keep the brains they evolved elsewhere. Training on multiple tracks produces more general drivers.
