How it works

Every genome is the full set of weights and biases of a feed-forward network (401 → 32 → 24 → 1) that scores a chess position. On its turn, a player tries every legal move, scores the resulting position with its network, and simply plays the highest-scoring move — no search tree, no lookahead.

The population pairs off into simultaneous games (64 genomes → 32 boards). Each generation plays two rounds — a seeded random pairing and a rank-adjacent pairing — with colors swapped inside each round, so every genome plays 4 games under comparable conditions.

Fitness is dominated by wins, with material advantage, survival, and game length as small tie-breaking shaping terms. Selection uses rank weighting; the next generation mixes exact elites, a protected global champion, champion variants, blended crossover children, Gaussian mutants, and fresh random immigrants. Mutation pressure automatically rises when evolution stagnates. There is no backpropagation, gradient, value function, or replay buffer anywhere.

The network sees the board as six signed piece planes — one 64-square plane per piece type (pawn, knight, bishop, rook, queen, king) holding +1 for its own piece and −1 for the opponent's, always oriented with its own home rank at the bottom — plus 17 engineered inputs: castling rights, material balance, pawn advancement on each half, the opponent's last two moves, a check flag, and game progress.

The "Is it learning?" chart is the clearest progress signal: after every generation the champion plays four quick games against a purely random legal-move player. Untrained genomes hover around 50%; a genuinely learning population climbs toward 100% with checkmates replacing material adjudications in the outcome chart.
